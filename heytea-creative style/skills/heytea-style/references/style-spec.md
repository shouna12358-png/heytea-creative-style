# Visual specification

## Canvas and product

- Use a static 9:16 vertical canvas with a pure white `#FFFFFF` background. Do not use cream, beige, warm gray, gradients, paper texture, or off-white.
- Keep the photorealistic product as the largest focal point, usually 55–70% of the useful area.
- Use a faint natural contact shadow. Avoid sets, gradients, patterns, borders, badges, UI panels, and glossy ad effects.
- Preserve container silhouette and rim, perspective, proportions, ingredient layers, topping placement, sauce distribution, irregularities, and color relationships.
- Allow only restrained cleanup: remove unrelated surroundings, balance exposure, clarify edges, and improve color without inventing ingredients.
- Never replace the food with an idealized product, illustration, 3D render, or different container.

## High-coverage narrative doodle

- Choose one visually dominant component whose shape suggests a story.
- Draw across much of that component while leaving the photographed surface visible.
- Reuse existing edges, folds, stacks, slices, and garnish as structural parts of the drawing.
- Use primitive black lines: shaky, uneven, slightly broken, blunt, and hand-drawn.
- Keep one coherent story. Avoid pasted-on mascots, polished illustration, complex faces, filled bodies, or unrelated decoration.

## Supporting marks and figures

- Add only a few steam lines, sparks, stars, speed strokes, wobble marks, crumbs, droplets, or impact curves.
- Use at most one or two functional, faceless micro-workers with clumsy limbs and simple torsos.
- Keep every supporting element secondary and preserve generous empty space.

## Color and mood

- Let the food provide nearly all color.
- Keep doodles and titles black.
- Use at most one tiny ingredient-derived accent color if necessary.
- Aim for clean, energetic, playful, slightly absurd, and appetizing.
